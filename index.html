Final Reflection

As I went through the design process for both visualization I tried to remove any bias I had while I was analyzing the data. Additionally, having to score how deceptive or earnest my design decisions were helped me realize that as I performed my data analysis I was still biased. When I scored visualizations that supported my proposition I tended to score more positively, compared to the visualizations that opposed my proposition. Overall, designing visualizations to persuade readers that my proposition is true was more difficult than designing visualizations to persuade readers that my proposition is false. It was difficult to not manipulate data too much to aid my efforts to persuade readers that my proposition is true. Moreover, it was difficult to come up with a proposition when I had to simultaneously think about how I would disprove my proposition. I was surprised at how easy it is to use the same dataset and come up with a different conclusion. It enlightened me on how easy it is to manipulate data to benefit your preconceived viewpoint on a manner. Creating both visualizations demonstrated that the person analyzing the data has a lot of power, and can choose to use it ethically or not. I now define “ethical analysis and visualization” as the practice of not drastically altering the dataset to benefit your implicit or explicit bias. Making sure to responsibly use your skills to accurately represent any relationships in the dataset. It is hard to draw bounds to distinguish “acceptable” persuasive choices vs. “misleading” ones, since I sometimes made similar design choices for both visualizations. For instance I decided to truncate the y-axis for scatter plot for my opposition visualization to deceive the readers, however, I had to truncate the y-axis for the upside down bar graph to avoid detracting from my point. Therefore, I draw the bounds of “acceptable” persuasive choices by allowing the removal of outliers that can skew the data. However, removing half the data that would indicate a different trend would be categorized as a “misleading” choices. Ultimately, to distinguish between the different design choices a person needs to recognize whether their analysis or trend of their data fundamentally changes from their design choice. If the design choice enhances then I would consider that an “acceptable” persuasive choice. However, if the design choice changes the interpretation of the dataset then I would consider that a “misleading” persuasive choice. It is open to the discretion of the data analyst to be able to distinguish between a design choice that changes the trend of the dataset or enhances the trend present in the dataset.
